What Critical Thinking Means for Design Students

Key Takeaways

  • Critical thinking in design is already embedded in the design process itself, through analyzing, challenging, deciding, and maintaining tensions between alternatives.
  • Making it visible is the challenge. It often hides in rubric language and process documentation, and needs to be made explicit not just in assessment, but in learning goals and activities.
  • Real-world contact as one of the key conditions — access to actual users and stakeholders creates the friction that critical thinking needs to develop.
  • Design education may be uniquely well-positioned to cultivate critical thinking, but the group questioned whether it survives into professional practice and how can we make it transferrable across scenarios and disciplines.
  • AI may be able to assist many parts of the design process, but maintaining coherence and tension across decisions over time may remain a distinctly human capacity.

Activity Format

Five participants gathered for a 90-minute breakout session exploring what critical thinking means in design education, and how it can be recognised, fostered, and evaluated in the classroom.

The session opened with a first round of sharing: how does each person understand and experience critical thinking in a design context? A second round followed, focusing on a more practical question: how does critical thinking show up in the design process and how might we evaluate it?

The format was discussion-based, with sticky notes used to capture and organize ideas as they emerged.

Outcomes

How critical thinking can be fostered

Contact with the real world emerged as a key condition for critical thinking. Having access to real people, including users, stakeholders, and real-world scenarios, gives students the opportunity to test and interrogate their design solutions.

How critical thinking can be evaluated

The group converged on the design process itself as the primary site for evaluating critical thinking. Key signals include:

  • Cycles and iterations: elongating the process matters; speed doesn't afford critical reflection.
  • Transparent AI use :even when generative AI is part of the process, students should still demonstrate iterative behavior and be transparent about how AI was used.
  • Showing prototypes: making the process visible is itself an act that fosters critical thinking.

How design education already fosters critical thinking

One participant identified four steps in the design process that inherently support critical thinking:

  1. Analyze the existing: what is already out there?
  2. Explore and challenge alternatives: what is missing, and what can be broken or questioned?
  3. Argue for decisions: why this direction and not another?
  4. Maintain tensions between alternatives: holding competing values in productive tension rather than resolving them prematurely.

This led to a broader discussion about whether critical thinking is discipline-specific. Design education's constant demand for explanation and justification of decisions was seen as particularly generative. Comparing across disciplines — art, law, philosophy — the group landed on philosophy as the field that most explicitly cultivates critical thinking, and wondered whether insights from philosophy could be brought into design teaching more deliberately.

A related question emerged: is critical thinking transferable across disciplines? Some participants felt it was more universal, while others emphasised that in design, it needs to be applied within the specific logic of the design process, including knowing when, where, and how to engage with generative AI.

The group also raised what happens to critical thinking after graduation: how does it survive in practice, within organizations and industry? One participant offered a useful frame — constant reflection across four dimensions: self, organization, society, and the tools one uses.

How to make critical thinking visible in teaching

Critical thinking is often buried in rubric language — in phrases like "provide an argument," "explore alternatives," or "reflect on your positionality." The group felt it deserves to be made more explicit, not just in assessment criteria but in learning goals and learning activities themselves.

Concrete approaches mentioned:

  • Annotated portfolios: making process transparent through reflective documentation
  • Reflection on decision-making: building in structured moments to articulate why choices were made
  • Teaching a mindset, not a recipe: theory-inspired design as a way of resisting formulaic approaches
  • Changes in feedback culture: one challenge raised was that this generation of students is less accustomed to giving and receiving critique, not just from teachers but among peers

On the values beneath the conversation

Four values surfaced repeatedly throughout the session: critical thinking, agency, creativity, and harmony.

  • Critical thinking was linked closely to agency — one participant described it simply as "agency over their work."
  • Creativity was seen as necessary for exploring alternatives, while critical thinking drives the decision-making that follows.
  • Importantly, the group noted that harmonizing — reaching consensus or smoothing over tensions — is not the same as critical thinking.

A provocative thought to close: using the four steps above as a working framework, maintaining consistency and tension across those steps may be precisely what generative AI cannot yet do. That thread of coherence across a design process — the why behind each decision, held in relation to all the others — might be where human critical thinking remains most irreplaceable.

What can you do right now

  • Try the annotated portfolio. Ask students to document not just what they made, but the why in their decision making and alternatives considered. It makes critical thinking legible.
  • Feedback collective. Find low-stakes ways to build peer critique into your teaching. They may need more scaffolding around giving and receiving feedback than previous generations.
  • Ask students where and why AI fits in their process, beyond their AI statements. Shifting the question from compliance to reflection, keeping critical thinking at the center.

Future directions

  • Mapping the values beneath critical thinking. This session surfaced four values — critical thinking, agency, creativity, and harmony — but only briefly. One of the next steps would be to explore these more deliberately: what do these values mean to design educators, how do they relate to one another, and are there others that didn't surface in this conversation? Understanding the value landscape more fully could provide a stronger foundation for any curriculum or policy work that follows.
  • Auditing rubrics for critical thinking. Across design education programmes, critical thinking is likely present in assessment criteria, but often implicitly, folded into language about argumentation, reflection, or exploration. A structured audit of existing rubrics could reveal how consistently and explicitly critical thinking (and other values) are represented, and where the gaps are.
